## Session: Role of AI in Cardio Vascular Diseases

* **Speaker:** Dr. Ashutosh Biswas
* **Designation:** Professor of Medicine & Executive Director
* **Organization:** AIIMS Bhubaneswar

## 1.0 Introduction: The Convergence of Artificial Intelligence and Medicine

Dr. Ashutosh Biswas began the session by framing the application of Artificial Intelligence (AI) in medicine as a pivotal development poised to bring about a revolutionary change in clinical practice. He emphasized that the integration of AI is not merely an incremental improvement but a fundamental shift towards a new paradigm of healthcare. The core objective of this transformation, as he outlined, is to achieve a more accurate and error-free standard of care, encompassing everything from initial diagnosis to final treatment. Dr. Biswas's introduction established AI as a strategic tool designed to augment and strengthen clinical intelligence, setting the stage for a detailed exploration of its foundational roles in the medical field.

---

## 2.0 The Foundational Pillars of AI in Medical Practice

Dr. Biswas articulated that the transformative potential of AI in medicine can be understood through four foundational pillars that address the complete patient care lifecycle. These pillars—prediction, prevention, diagnosis, and therapeutics/prognosis—represent the key functional areas where AI is set to enhance clinical capabilities, moving from forecasting future health risks to tailoring individual treatment plans.

**2.1 Prediction**

The first pillar, prediction, leverages AI's capacity to process vast and complex datasets to forecast the likelihood of future diseases. Dr. Biswas drew an analogy to a "weather forecast" for health, where AI can analyze robust data inputs such as a patient's genetic makeup and biological markers. Through this analysis, it becomes possible to predict the potential onset of conditions like cancer, heart attack, or diabetes years before any clinical symptoms appear, offering an unprecedented look into an individual's long-term health trajectory.

**2.2 Prevention**

Flowing directly from prediction, the second pillar is prevention. Dr. Biswas explained that by identifying high-risk individuals long before a disease manifests, AI creates a critical window for proactive intervention. This predictive insight enables the implementation of targeted preventive strategies designed to mitigate risks and avert severe health events. In the context of cardiology, this could mean preventing a sudden cardiac attack or other serious complications by addressing underlying factors identified by the AI model.

**2.3 Diagnosis**

The third pillar focuses on AI's ability to enhance and accelerate diagnosis. The central goal, as identified by Dr. Biswas, is to achieve the earliest possible detection of disease, a factor he noted is the key to successfully curing conditions like cancer. AI systems can analyze medical images and other data to identify subtle pathological signals at a micro or cellular level that may be invisible to the human eye. This allows for the diagnosis of diseases at a much earlier stage than is possible through conventional methods alone.

**2.4 Therapeutics and Prognosis**

The final pillar addresses treatment and patient outcomes. Dr. Biswas described how AI is the engine behind "precision medicine" or "individualized medicine." By analyzing a patient's unique genomic, proteomic, and other biological markers, AI can help tailor the most effective treatment protocol for that specific individual. Furthermore, it can be used to forecast a patient's prognosis by assessing the likely success of a given treatment, predicting recovery percentages, and quantifying mortality risks, thus providing clinicians and patients with a clearer understanding of the potential therapeutic journey.

These four pillars illustrate the functional power of AI, which is built upon its unique ability to process information in ways that extend beyond natural human capabilities.

---

## 3.0 AI as an Extension of Clinical Intelligence

Dr. Biswas positioned AI not as a replacement for medical professionals but as a powerful extension of their inherent clinical intelligence. He framed AI's role as a tool to overcome the natural limitations of human perception and data processing, thereby augmenting the diagnostic and decision-making capabilities of physicians. This collaborative model is based on a synergy between natural and artificial intelligence, where AI’s power is tempered by the indispensable need for clinical correlation.

* Human Intelligence: Dr. Biswas explained that medical professionals are trained over many years to recognize patterns and make diagnoses using their senses and intellect. They learn to interpret how a patient is "walking and talking," recognizing that for a neurological problem, a patient will have "different gates," and an "exclusive gate" can point directly to the specific neurological issue. This process, which applies equally to analyzing a CT scan or an ECG, relies on the brain's ability to process visual information and correlate it with learned medical knowledge and context.
* Artificial Intelligence: In parallel, AI performs a similar function of pattern recognition but on a vastly expanded scale. He noted that AI can process immense volumes of data, helping minimize the human error that persists in medicine. It can also perceive details at a micro or cellular level that are imperceptible to the naked eye. However, Dr. Biswas provided a critical real-world example from the COVID-19 pandemic to illustrate AI's limitations without human oversight. During the pandemic, an AI model could identify "ground glass opacity" (GCC) on a CT scan with greater accuracy than an RTPCR test. Yet, post-pandemic, the same GCC signal was no longer a reliable indicator of COVID-19. This demonstrates that AI requires "clinical correlation" to be effective, as it lacks the contextual awareness to adapt to changing epidemiological landscapes.

This conceptual framework, where AI strengthens human expertise, finds a particularly urgent application in the high-stakes field of cardiology.

## 4.0 Focused Application: AI in Cardiovascular Disease (CVD)

The session then focused on the specific application of AI in cardiovascular disease, a strategic priority given its status as the leading cause of death globally. Dr. Biswas highlighted the critical need for early detection in CVD, as cardiac events are often sudden and catastrophic, leaving little time for intervention. AI offers a powerful set of tools to address this challenge by enabling earlier and more accurate risk assessment and diagnosis. The primary methods for this application include:

1. Image Processing: AI algorithms are trained to analyze a range of cardiovascular images, including the ECG, Echocardiogram, CT scan, MRI, and Angiography. By processing these images, AI can gain a comprehensive understanding of the heart's structural and functional condition, from electrical activity and valvular heart disease to the state of its musculature and vascular supply.
2. Risk Prediction: AI can compute specific parameters from images to predict future risk. Dr. Biswas noted that current models can assess coronary artery flow, arterial wall thickness, and calcium scores to identify existing blockages. He clarified the present limitations, stating, "at this moment we can predict really when the already 20% block or 10% block so we can see the block but we cannot predict that when there is no block we cannot say after 3 years whether you will have a block or not." The future of true prediction, he explained, lies in developing AI that can analyze the coronary artery wall at a "cellular level," assessing the "integrity of the intima medial thickness" long before a blockage forms.
3. Early Signal Detection: One of AI's most significant contributions is its ability to identify minute and subtle signals within complex data that a human observer might miss. For example, an AI model could detect faint patterns in an ECG that signal a future risk of electrical disturbances or other cardiac problems, providing a crucial early warning.

By applying these methods, AI transforms cardiovascular care from a reactive to a more predictive and preventive discipline, a shift with profound implications for public health.

## 5.0 Broader Societal Impact and Future Applications

Dr. Biswas extended the discussion beyond the clinic to address the broader societal and systemic transformations that AI can facilitate in healthcare. He outlined several future applications where AI is poised to enhance healthcare access, delivery, and innovation on a large scale.

* Bridging the Rural-Urban Healthcare Gap: AI-powered telemedicine platforms can make specialist care accessible to underserved populations. Medical images and data from patients in rural areas can be transmitted to urban centers, where AI tools can perform an initial analysis before review by a specialist, effectively closing the geographic gap in expertise.
* Remote Patient Monitoring: The combination of wearable devices and AI creates a system for continuous patient monitoring. These devices can track vital signs and other health metrics in real-time, with AI algorithms analyzing the data stream for any anomalies. If a potential emergency is detected, the system can automatically alert physicians, enabling immediate and potentially life-saving intervention.
* Robotic-Assisted Surgery: Dr. Biswas briefly noted the growing role of AI in robotic surgery. AI contributes to greater precision and control during surgical procedures, which can lead to improved patient outcomes, reduced complications, and faster recovery times.
* Accelerating Drug Discovery: AI can significantly streamline and accelerate the lengthy and expensive process of drug discovery. By analyzing complex biological data, AI models can identify promising drug candidates and predict their efficacy, reducing the time and resources needed to bring new medications to market.

For these visionary applications to become a widespread reality, however, a number of significant challenges must first be addressed.

## 6.0 Challenges and Key Considerations for AI Adoption

While acknowledging the immense promise of AI, Dr. Biswas also provided a pragmatic overview of the significant hurdles that must be overcome for its successful and ethical integration into mainstream medical practice. The path to widespread adoption is complex, involving technical, logistical, and societal challenges.

* Validation and Reliability: It is imperative to rigorously validate AI models to ensure their diagnostic and predictive outputs are consistently accurate and dependable across diverse patient populations.
* Integration: A major logistical challenge is integrating new AI tools into existing clinical workflows. Dr. Biswas noted that the healthcare industry is "very segmented," making it difficult to create a "seamless flow of data from one specialist to another" when addressing comorbid conditions that affect multiple organ systems.
* Trust and Acceptance: Building trust is crucial for adoption. This involves the difficult job of creating "faith and believe in the community," ensuring that both the medical profession and the public develop confidence in the reliability of AI-based recommendations.
* Ethical and Privacy Concerns: The use of AI raises critical ethical questions, paramount among them being the need to keep sensitive patient health data confidential and secure.
* Cost and Resource Allocation: The development, implementation, and maintenance of sophisticated AI technologies require substantial financial investment, posing a challenge for resource allocation within healthcare systems.
* The Need for Interdisciplinary Collaboration: A fundamental obstacle is the "huge gap in the interface between the medical and engineering" domains. Dr. Biswas stressed that future progress depends on clinicians and engineers sitting together to bridge this gap, allowing medical needs to inform engineering solutions.

Dr. Biswas concluded this section by reinforcing that AI is fundamentally a clinical decision support system. Its purpose is to augment and support physicians, not to operate with full autonomy.

---

## 7.0 Conclusion: AI as a Clinical Decision Support System

Dr. Biswas's core message is that the primary role of AI in medicine is to serve as a powerful clinical decision support system, designed not to replace physicians but to augment their clinical intelligence. By processing vast amounts of data with a level of detail beyond human perception, AI can help reduce human error, refine diagnostic accuracy, and ultimately enhance medical practice. However, realizing AI's ultimate vision of predictive medicine requires a fundamental paradigm shift. As Dr. Biswas explained, this involves moving beyond analyzing data from sick patients who arrive at hospitals and instead reaching "to the community healthy people" to build predictive models from the ground up. Achieving this transformative goal, he concluded, is contingent upon fostering deep, interdisciplinary collaboration between the medical and engineering domains, ensuring that technological innovation is driven by clinical need to deliver a higher standard of care for all.
