## Session: Correlating Morphological Features of Gestational Sac and Early Embryo in Predicting First Trimester Pregnancy Loss

**Speaker:** Dr. Abhinav R Dasari

**Designation:** Department of Obstetrics & Gynecology

**Organization:** Kalinga Institute of Medical Sciences (KIMS), Bhubaneswar

**Topic:**  Correlating Morphological Features of Gestational Sac and Early Embryo in Predicting First Trimester Pregnancy Loss

**1.1. Foundational Concepts of Early Pregnancy Ultrasound**
To understand the application of Artificial Intelligence in early pregnancy assessment, it is first necessary to grasp the fundamental biological structures and measurements observed during a first-trimester ultrasound. These anatomical features and their corresponding metrics represent the core data points that any AI model would need to analyze to provide meaningful clinical insights.
Dr. Dasari provided clear definitions for the key structures observed during these initial scans:
• Embryo: The earliest stage of human development following fertilization, before it develops into a fetus.
• Gestational Sac: The first visible sign of a pregnancy on an ultrasound. It is the fluid-filled structure located within the uterus that contains the developing embryo.
• Yolk Sac: A structure located within the gestational sac that provides essential nutrition to the embryo in its early stages.
These structures form the basis for the specific, standardized measurements that are taken to monitor the progression and viability of the pregnancy.

**1.2. Key Sonographic Measurements and Their Clinical Significance**
Standardized measurements are the cornerstone of early pregnancy assessment, providing the quantitative data used to date the pregnancy accurately and evaluate its health. These metrics allow clinicians to compare fetal development against established growth patterns, identifying potential discrepancies that may signal a problem.
Dr. Dasari detailed the following standard measurements taken during first-trimester sonography:
• Mean Sac Diameter (MSD): This is a primary measurement for dating a pregnancy, calculated by taking the average of the gestational sac's height, length, and width. According to Dr. Dasari, a normal gestational sac increases in size at a rate of approximately 1 mm per day.
• Crown Rump Length (CRL): Identified by Dr. Dasari as the "gold standard" for dating a pregnancy, the CRL is a measurement taken in a straight line from the top of the embryo's head to the bottom of its trunk (cordal end).
• Yolk Sac Diameter: This is a measurement of the largest inner diameter of the yolk sac, measured carefully avoiding the surrounding echogenic (brightly reflective) margins.
• Fetal Heart Rate: The embryonic heart rate is measured using a specialized "M-mode" (Motion mode) on the ultrasound machine, which detects the motion of the heart to calculate its rate.
While these measurements are fundamental, the process of acquiring them is not without its challenges, which introduces variability and subjectivity.

**1.3. Challenges and Subjectivity in Current Ultrasound Practice**
Dr. Dasari pinpointed three fundamental sources of variability in manual sonography—operator, machine, and technique—that collectively undermine the consistency required for reliable diagnostics. These challenges form the direct impetus for introducing AI-driven standardization.
Dr. Dasari highlighted several key issues encountered in routine practice:
1. Observer Bias: Different operators may have varying interpretations of anatomical boundaries or use the measurement calipers on the ultrasound machine differently. This leads to inconsistent measurements between sonographers, even when scanning the same patient.
2. Difficulty in Standardization: Consistency is difficult to achieve across different ultrasound machines, which may have varying resolutions and settings. This, combined with differences between patients and operators, complicates efforts to regularize the measurement process.
3. Frame Selection: A live ultrasound is a video feed. The operator must pause this feed at the "perfect frame" to take an accurate measurement. Choosing this ideal static image from a dynamic video stream is a subjective skill that develops with experience and can be a significant source of variability.
These challenges form the basis for proposing AI as a tool to overcome human- and machine-based inconsistencies.

**1.4. The Proposed Role of Artificial Intelligence**
Artificial intelligence is introduced not as a replacement for skilled clinicians but as a powerful assistive tool to enhance precision, consistency, and predictive capability in early pregnancy screening. By automating and standardizing key tasks, AI has the potential to make the assessment process more objective and efficient.
Dr. Dasari outlined several ways AI can address the current challenges:
• Reduce Observer Bias: AI can be trained to regularize and standardize measurements according to a fixed set of rules, removing the variability introduced by different human operators.
• Enable Autobiometry: This refers to automated measurements conducted by the AI model using fixed, pre-defined parameters. This would ensure that structures are measured the same way every time, regardless of the operator.
• Develop Predictive Models: An AI model could be developed with the following capabilities:
    ◦ Automatically identify key structures like the gestational sac and yolk sac, and assess their morphology (shape, location, thickness).
    ◦ Measure critical parameters, including CRL, various diameters, and fetal heart rate.
    ◦ Classify pregnancies as normal or abnormal based on a comprehensive analysis of these features.
    ◦ Predict future risks and complications such as high blood pressure or high sugars that can arise later in the pregnancy, providing a quantitative risk score in percentages.
Data Requirements for Model Development
To successfully train such a model, Dr. Dasari proposed a standardized approach to data collection. He suggested using short video clips, typically 15 to 30 seconds but up to a maximum of one minute, captured at a minimum resolution of 1920x1080 pixels and a frame rate of at least 30 frames per second. This standardized format would provide the AI with consistent, high-quality data for processing.
While the potential advantages are significant, a balanced perspective requires acknowledging the practical hurdles that remain.

**1.5 Advantages and Limitations of AI Integration**

While Artificial Intelligence offers the potential to revolutionize early pregnancy assessment, its practical implementation faces several technical, logistical, and regulatory hurdles.

**Advantages**

- Reduces observer bias by standardizing measurements  
- Eases high-volume patient screening through automation  
- Provides quantitative measurements instead of subjective qualitative assessments  
- Supports less experienced sonographers by offering consistent guidance  

**Limitations**

- Dependence on video quality and lack of standardization  
- Difficulty integrating AI systems with older ultrasound machines  
- Concerns regarding clinical accuracy versus human judgment  
- Requirement for government and regulatory approvals  
- High cost of required software and hardware  

Ultimately, Dr. Dasari concluded that AI should be viewed as a **support tool** designed to **augment, not replace**, the invaluable expertise and clinical judgment of trained practitioners.
